## Pattern A — link included at the end of the post

Our design studio in Tokyo gets paid in vegetables.

Not as a joke — it's in the contract.

At BOATship, we run what we call an "equal exchange compensation" model. Alongside standard fees, we accept payment for design, PR, and production work in other forms of value: skills, workspace, services, goods.

It started with a sentence we kept hearing from regional producers and startups in Japan: "We'd love to work with you, but we don't have the budget." The passion was real. The project was worth doing. Money was the only thing missing — and that felt like the wrong reason to walk away.

How it works in practice:

🥬 A farm pays for its website with a monthly box of fresh vegetables.
🏢 A shared-office brand traded co-working space for its branding work.
📷 A photographer exchanged product shoots for web design.

The interesting part isn't the barter itself. It's how the relationship changes when money leaves the room. No one is the client; no one is the vendor. The only test for a fair trade is a question both sides answer honestly: "Am I genuinely satisfied with this exchange?"

If you know the Japanese phrase *o-tagai-sama* — roughly, "we're in this together" — that's the cultural instinct behind it. Japan's business culture can seem rigid from the outside, but there is also this: a long tradition of reciprocity that doesn't always route through money.

Six months in, we've learned that trades like this take more conversation up front — and produce longer relationships after. We published the full story, including how we define "equal," in English:
https://www.boatship.jp/news/equal-exchange-compensation-launch/?utm_source=linkedin&utm_medium=social&utm_campaign=pr01_equal_exchange&utm_content=en

(More about who we are, in English: https://www.boatship.jp/en/about/)

If you're exploring the Japanese market: what could your company trade, other than money?
